# Interview Prep

## Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)
I'm an operations generalist with 6 years of experience building internal systems and processes. Most recently, I was the Operations Manager at a 50-person B2B SaaS startup where I built our onboarding process from scratch, automated manual workflows, and managed vendor relationships that saved the company $34K a year. Before that, I spent 3 years in consulting doing process improvement and requirements gathering for healthcare and retail clients. I'm looking for a remote ops role where I can own the systems that keep a growing team running.

## STAR Stories

### Story 1: "The Onboarding Overhaul That Nobody Wanted to Own"
- **Situation:** When I joined NovaTech, new employee onboarding took 12 days on average. It was managed through a shared Google Doc that nobody kept updated. New hires regularly showed up on day one without laptop access, and IT requests were getting lost in Slack threads. The company was hiring 2–3 people a month, and every onboarding was a mini fire drill.
- **Task:** My VP asked me to "fix onboarding" with no additional budget and no additional headcount. I had to design a system that 7 department leads would actually use — and none of them had asked for a new process.
- **Action:** I interviewed each department lead to understand their specific onboarding steps. Then I built a structured system in Airtable with department-specific views, automated Slack reminders via Zapier for each milestone, and auto-generated IT setup tickets. The hardest part was change management — I ran 4 feedback rounds and had to compromise on several design decisions to get buy-in. I also wrote a one-page guide for each department lead so they didn't have to learn Airtable from scratch.
- **Result:** Onboarding time dropped from 12 days to 5 days. New hire satisfaction for "onboarding experience" went from 3.2/5 to 4.4/5. The system was still running when I left 20 months later, with zero modifications needed. Every department adopted it.
- **Best for:** "Tell me about a project you built from scratch," "Give me an example of driving change without authority," "How do you handle stakeholders who resist new processes?"

### Story 2: "The Vendor Nobody Knew Was Failing"
- **Situation:** NovaTech worked with 8 third-party vendors, but nobody tracked whether they were meeting their SLAs. The IT support vendor was a constant source of complaints in Slack — slow response times, unresolved tickets — but leadership assumed it was "just the nature of IT support." There was no data to prove otherwise.
- **Task:** I needed to create visibility into vendor performance so we could make informed decisions about renewals instead of auto-renewing every contract.
- **Action:** I built an SLA tracking dashboard in Airtable by pulling data from email threads, support ticket logs, and invoices — none of this was centralized before. I set up a monthly vendor review meeting and started presenting the data to leadership. When the dashboard showed the IT vendor was missing SLAs 40% of the time, I led the conversation about replacing them. I also renegotiated contracts with 2 other vendors by using competitive quotes as leverage.
- **Result:** Replaced the underperforming IT vendor — the new vendor had a 92% SLA compliance rate in its first quarter. Saved $34,000/year total across 3 renegotiated contracts ($18K from cloud hosting alone). Leadership started using the dashboard for all vendor decisions.
- **Best for:** "Tell me about a time you identified a problem nobody else saw," "How do you use data to drive decisions?", "Give me an example of saving the company money"

### Story 3: "The $200K Licensing Catch"
- **Situation:** During a routine process mapping exercise at Lakefront Health (a healthcare client at Meridian), I was documenting the technology stack used by the claims processing department. While mapping their tools, I noticed they were paying for an EHR module that the billing department also had a separate license for.
- **Task:** It wasn't my job to audit software costs — I was there for process mapping. But I flagged it because it seemed like a significant overlap.
- **Action:** I documented the overlap in my process map deliverable and raised it in our weekly client status meeting. I showed both departments' tool inventories side-by-side and asked whether the licenses could be consolidated. The client's IT director confirmed it was a duplicate — two departments had each procured the module independently 3 years earlier, and nobody had caught it.
- **Result:** The client consolidated the licenses and saved approximately $200,000/year. It wasn't technically part of our engagement scope, but the client specifically mentioned it in their satisfaction survey, and our engagement manager used it as a case study for future healthcare proposals.
- **Best for:** "Tell me about a time you went above and beyond," "Give me an example of attention to detail," "How do you add value beyond your defined role?"

### Story 4: "Managing Competing Stakeholders at Lakefront Health"
- **Situation:** During the claims processing overhaul at Lakefront Health, I was running requirements workshops with three groups that wanted very different things. Claims adjusters wanted a simpler interface with fewer fields. Supervisors wanted more audit controls and approval steps. IT wanted to minimize custom development and stick to out-of-the-box functionality.
- **Task:** I needed to produce a single BRD that all three groups would sign off on, within a 6-week timeline.
- **Action:** I stopped running combined workshops after the second session devolved into arguments. Instead, I ran separate sessions with each group to fully understand their priorities. Then I created a requirements priority matrix that mapped each requirement to a stakeholder group and classified it as "must-have," "important," or "nice-to-have." I presented the matrix back to all three groups in a combined session, showing where there was alignment (about 70% of requirements) and where there were trade-offs. For the trade-offs, I facilitated a discussion where each group explained their reasoning, and we reached compromises on 90% of the disputes.
- **Result:** Delivered the BRD with 147 documented requirements on time. All three stakeholder groups signed off. The engagement manager later told me it was the smoothest multi-stakeholder requirements phase he'd seen in 2 years.
- **Best for:** "Tell me about a time you dealt with conflicting stakeholders," "How do you handle disagreements at work?", "Describe a time you had to influence without authority"

## Common Questions — Prepared Answers

### "Why are you leaving / did you leave your last role?"
**Real reason:** NovaTech did a round of layoffs in January 2026 cutting ~30% of staff. The entire ops team was eliminated except for one coordinator. My VP was also let go. It was a financial decision, not performance-related.

**Interview-ready answer:** "NovaTech went through a significant restructuring in January — they reduced headcount by about 30%, and the operations team was heavily impacted. My VP and I were both part of the reduction. It wasn't performance-related — I'd received strong reviews and had just completed a major vendor management initiative — but the company needed to cut costs. I'm treating it as an opportunity to find a role where I can have even more impact, ideally in a remote environment where I can focus on building operational systems."

### "Tell me about yourself"
"I'm an operations generalist — I've spent the last 6 years building processes and internal tools, first as a business analyst in consulting and then as an operations manager at a B2B SaaS startup. At NovaTech, I built our employee onboarding system from scratch, automated our expense reconciliation process, and managed vendor relationships that saved the company $34K a year. Before that, at Meridian Consulting, I ran requirements workshops and did process mapping for healthcare and retail clients — that's where I developed my ability to sit in a room with 15 stakeholders who all want different things and come out with a plan everyone signs off on. I also ran my own small e-commerce business for a year between those roles, which taught me a lot about wearing every hat. I'm looking for a remote ops role at a growing company where I can own the systems that keep things running."

### "What's your biggest weakness?"
"My technical skills have a ceiling. I can build automations in Airtable and Zapier all day, and I've written basic Python scripts for data processing, but I'm not an engineer. If a problem requires real software development — writing production code, building APIs, managing infrastructure — I need to partner with someone who can do that. I've learned to be upfront about that boundary because it helps me focus on what I'm actually good at, which is the process design and stakeholder side, and bring in the right technical people when the solution needs to go deeper."

### "Why do you want to work here?" (Framework)
Structure: (1) Something specific about the company's product or mission that connects to my experience, (2) how the role matches what I'm looking for — owning operational systems at a growing company, (3) something about the team or culture that I noticed in the job posting or research. Avoid generic statements. Research the company before every interview.

### "What happened with your e-commerce business?"
"I ran a small curated home goods business for about a year after college. I learned a ton — sourcing, Shopify, marketing, fulfillment, managing a P&L — but ultimately the margins in low-price-point physical goods were too thin to sustain as a one-person operation. I made the decision to close it and move into a corporate role where I could apply those operational skills at scale. I don't regret the experience — it gave me a hands-on understanding of running a business end-to-end that I wouldn't have gotten any other way — but I'm honest that it wasn't a financial success."

- "I saved my last company $34K a year just by actually tracking whether our vendors were doing what they said they would."
- "My superpower is getting 7 department leads who didn't ask for a new process to adopt one anyway — and keep using it."
- "I'm not an engineer. I'm the person who figures out what needs to be built and makes sure it actually gets used."
- "The best operations work is invisible — things just work, and nobody has to think about why."
